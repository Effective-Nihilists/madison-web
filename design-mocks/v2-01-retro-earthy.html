    Reading the Major Arcana: A Beginner's Field Guide

    by Madison · 12 min read

    The Major Arcana are the twenty-two cards that carry the big, mythic weather of a tarot deck — the kind of cards that make people gasp at parties for no reason. They're not fortune-telling so much as a mirror with very dramatic lighting. This guide is for the absolute beginner who bought a deck on impulse and is now staring at it nervously across the room.

    Before we start: there is no wrong way to do this. The cards do not have feelings about your technique. Light a candle, get comfortable, and let the symbols do the talking.

    What the Major Arcana actually is

    Think of the Majors as the spine of the deck — the recurring characters of a story about becoming a whole person. The numbered journey runs from The Fool (pure beginning) all the way to The World (completion), and every card in between is a chapter.

    Why beginners should start here

    Because they're loud. The imagery is rich and the meanings are archetypal, which means your intuition has a lot to grab onto before you've memorized a single keyword.

    "The cards don't tell you the future. They tell you what you already half-know but haven't said out loud yet."

    What you'll need

    • a candle (any candle — the dramatic flicker is the point)
    • a journal to write down what you see
    • your deck (any deck you like the art of)
    • one quiet hour with the phone face-down
    "Start with the cards that scare you. They're usually the ones with the most to say."

    The first three cards, briefly

    1. The Fool — the leap before the look; a fresh start with everything still possible and nothing yet decided.
    2. The Magician — you already have every tool you need on the table; this card is about using them.
    3. The High Priestess — the quiet inner voice; secrets, intuition, and the things known but not spoken.
    Herb pairing: mugwort. Traditionally burned or sipped as a weak tea before a reading to encourage vivid intuition and dreams. (Don't overdo it. Reginald the moth is not a reliable dosing consultant.)

    Where to go next

    Pull one card a morning for a week and write a single sentence about it. By Sunday you'll have a small private dictionary that means more to you than anything a book could hand you.
